## 1) Contextualized Privacy Defense for LLM Agents
- **Authors:** Yule Wen, Yanzhe Zhang, Jianxun Lian, Xiaoyuan Yi, Xing Xie, Diyi Yang  
- **arXiv:** `2603.02983v1` (**cs.CR; cs.AI; cs.CL**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Proposes Contextualized Defense Instructing (CDI), where a dedicated instructor model emits step-specific privacy guidance during agent execution.
- Moves beyond static guard prompts by making privacy intervention contextual and proactive at each decision point.
- Trains the instructor with RL over failure trajectories containing privacy violations.
- Formalizes intervention points in the canonical agent loop and compares CDI with baseline defenses.
- Reports a better privacy-helpfulness trade-off (94.2% privacy preservation, 80.6% helpfulness) than baselines.
- Shows stronger robustness under adversarial settings than passive guarding approaches.

**Novelty:**
- Privacy defense as a *dynamic co-policy* rather than static policy text.
- RL from privacy-failure trajectories to optimize intervention behavior.

**Security relevance:**
- **Permissions/policy + data privacy**: directly addresses real-time data release decisions in tool-using agents.
- Useful for preventing context-dependent exfiltration paths that static filters miss.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Simulation-heavy evaluation; transfer to production agents is unclear.
- Risk of over-blocking in ambiguous contexts.
- Instructor model itself may become an attack surface.

**Tags:** `agent-guardrails`, `privacy`, `policy-intervention`, `reinforcement-learning`, `adversarial-robustness`

## 2) Beyond Task Completion: Revealing Corrupt Success in LLM Agents through Procedure-Aware Evaluation
- **Authors:** Hongliu Cao, Ilias Driouich, Eoin Thomas  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03116v1` (**cs.AI**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Introduces Procedure-Aware Evaluation (PAE) to assess *how* agents act, not only whether final task goals are met.
- Encodes procedure constraints over observation, communication, and execution consistency.
- Scores agents along utility, efficiency, interaction quality, and procedural integrity with multi-axis gating.
- Finds 27–78% of benchmark “successes” are actually corrupt outcomes with policy/procedure violations.
- Shows Pass@k and leaderboard rankings shift significantly when procedural integrity gates are applied.
- Diagnoses model-specific failure signatures (faithfulness/compliance vs execution errors).

**Novelty:**
- Formalized corrupt-success concept with disqualifying procedural gates.
- Multi-axis compliance framing that exposes benchmark reward misalignment.

**Security relevance:**
- **Permissions/policy**: catches silent policy bypass hidden behind successful end states.
- Critical for authorization-sensitive agents where process correctness matters as much as outcome.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Depends on quality/completeness of procedure specs.
- May increase eval complexity and annotation burden.

**Tags:** `agent-evaluation`, `policy-faithfulness`, `procedural-integrity`, `benchmarking`, `governance`

## 3) REGAL: A Registry-Driven Architecture for Deterministic Grounding of Agentic AI in Enterprise Telemetry
- **Authors:** Yuvraj Agrawal  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03018v1` (**cs.AI; cs.SE**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Presents an architecture that grounds agents on enterprise telemetry through deterministic, versioned computation.
- Uses a medallion ELT pipeline to produce replayable “Gold” artifacts instead of raw event streams.
- Compiles declarative metric definitions into MCP tools via a registry layer.
- Constrains LLM actions to a bounded, version-controlled interface-as-code surface.
- Claims improvements in operational governance and reduced tool drift.
- Positioning is architectural rather than algorithmic, targeting practical enterprise deployability.

**Novelty:**
- Registry-as-compilation layer for semantic/tool interface alignment.
- Deterministic telemetry computation as first-class primitive for agent grounding.

**Security relevance:**
- **Permissions/least privilege + auditability**: bounded tool surface and versioned interfaces reduce overbroad access.
- Helps enforce governance and post-hoc auditing in enterprise agent workflows.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Limited evidence on adversarial prompt-injection resilience.
- Integration complexity for organizations lacking mature telemetry pipelines.

**Tags:** `enterprise-agents`, `deterministic-grounding`, `mcp-tools`, `governance`, `least-privilege`

## 4) PrivMedChat: End-to-End Differentially Private RLHF for Medical Dialogue Systems
- **Authors:** Sudip Bhujel  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03054v1` (**cs.CL**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Proposes an end-to-end DP-RLHF pipeline for medical dialogue systems.
- Applies DP-SGD to SFT, reward model training, and PPO actor/critic when dialogue-derived data is used.
- Introduces annotation-free preference pair construction from physician vs filtered non-expert responses.
- Reports strongest ROUGE-L among compared DP baselines at ε=7.
- Shows reduced hallucination/harmful advice and near-chance membership-inference AUC.
- Targets practical privacy-preserving alignment in sensitive healthcare contexts.

**Novelty:**
- Full-stack DP treatment across RLHF stages, not only SFT.
- Scalable preference construction without clinician annotation.

**Security relevance:**
- **Data privacy/governance**: mitigates training-data leakage and membership inference risks.
- Blueprint for compliance-focused alignment in regulated domains.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Utility/privacy trade-offs may vary across model scales.
- Domain transfer outside medical dialogue remains untested.

**Tags:** `differential-privacy`, `rlhf`, `medical-llm`, `membership-inference`, `data-governance`

## 5) A Multi-Turn Framework for Evaluating AI Misuse in Fraud and Cybercrime Scenarios (v2 update)
- **Authors:** Kimberly T. Mai et al.  
- **arXiv:** `2602.21831v2` (**cs.CY**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Develops expert-grounded multi-turn misuse evaluations for romance scams, CEO fraud, and identity theft scenarios.
- Focuses on whether models provide actionable uplift beyond publicly available web info.
- Finds current models give limited actionable abuse help without advanced jailbreak effort.
- Shows safeguards materially reduce misuse assistance.
- Reveals benign-seeming decomposition can elicit more harmful guidance than explicit malicious framing.
- Provides a reproducible framework for longitudinal risk tracking.

**Novelty:**
- Realistic multi-turn misuse protocol co-designed with law-enforcement/policy experts.
- Measures incremental criminal utility rather than binary safety labels.

**Security relevance:**
- **Policy/access control**: tests robustness of refusal policies under conversational decomposition attacks.
- Useful for red-teaming agent workflows that chain harmless sub-queries into harmful plans.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Text-only scope; multimodal/tool-augmented abuse not covered.
- “Actionability” judgments may vary by evaluator cohort.

**Tags:** `misuse-evaluation`, `fraud`, `cybercrime`, `multi-turn-safety`, `jailbreak-resilience`

## 6) Understanding and Mitigating Dataset Corruption in LLM Steering
- **Authors:** Cullen Anderson et al.  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03206v1` (**cs.LG; cs.AI; cs.CL**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Studies robustness of contrastive steering when steering datasets are noisy or adversarially corrupted.
- Finds moderate corruption is tolerated, but non-trivial poisoning induces targeted side effects.
- Analyzes geometric structure of corruption in high-dimensional activation space.
- Replaces naive mean estimation with robust mean estimators to reduce malicious influence.
- Demonstrates mitigation of many unwanted steering behaviors.
- Highlights safety risks in low-cost, inference-time control methods.

**Novelty:**
- First focused corruption-robustness study for contrastive steering pipelines.
- Practical robust-statistics mitigation with minimal pipeline changes.

**Security relevance:**
- **Policy/capability control**: steering vectors can encode behavior constraints; poisoning undermines this control channel.
- Relevant to secure post-training controls in agent deployments.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Evaluated on selected corruption regimes; adaptive attackers may evolve.
- Impact on capability/safety trade-off at scale not fully mapped.

**Tags:** `steering`, `data-poisoning`, `robust-statistics`, `alignment-control`, `inference-safety`

## 7) RAIN: Secure and Robust Aggregation under Shuffle Model of Differential Privacy
- **Authors:** Yuhang Li et al.  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03108v1` (**cs.CR**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Proposes RAIN, a framework for robust and verifiable aggregation under Shuffle-DP.
- Uses sign-space aggregation to preserve robustness despite local noise and anonymization.
- Introduces secret-shared protocols for shuffling/aggregation with tight Shuffle-DP guarantees.
- Adds per-round verification to detect selective dropping and tampering.
- Reports strong poisoning resistance with low overhead and substantial communication/runtime gains.
- Targets privacy-preserving federated settings where shuffler trust is limited.

**Novelty:**
- Joint treatment of privacy, poisoning robustness, and integrity verification in one protocol stack.
- Efficient malicious-security checks under shuffle-based anonymity.

**Security relevance:**
- **Data security/privacy + integrity/audit**: defends training pipelines from poisoning and aggregator tampering.
- Indirectly relevant to secure agent-model update ecosystems.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Not LLM-agent specific; transfer to frontier LLM training scale is unclear.
- Real-world deployment assumptions on client behavior need testing.

**Tags:** `shuffle-dp`, `secure-aggregation`, `poisoning-defense`, `federated-learning`, `verifiability`

## 9) From Language to Action: Can LLM-Based Agents Be Used for Embodied Robot Cognition?
- **Authors:** Shinas Shaji et al.  
- **arXiv:** `2603.03148v1` (**cs.RO**)

**Summary (5–8 lines):**
- Proposes an embodied architecture where an LLM agent plans while working and episodic memory modules support adaptation.
- Connects language planning to high-level robotic tools for perception/navigation/manipulation.
- Evaluates object-placement and object-swapping tasks in household simulation.
- Shows memory-guided adaptation can improve task behavior in structured settings.
- Also reports major failures: hallucinated success states and poor sequential instruction compliance.
- Demonstrates practical memory-loop benefits and safety fragility in embodied control.

**Novelty:**
- Integrates explicit working+episodic memory with tool-using embodied LLM control.
- Empirical analysis of memory utilization alongside failure dynamics.

**Security relevance:**
- **Memory access/lifecycle**: memory modules influence action correctness and safety-critical persistence.
- Highlights need for trusted state validation before action execution.

**Limitations / open questions:**
- Simulation scope is narrow; real-world robustness uncertain.
- Limited treatment of adversarial environment inputs.

**Tags:** `embodied-agents`, `episodic-memory`, `tool-use`, `robot-safety`, `state-validation`

## 10-Line Trend Summary (with research gaps)
1. The strongest thread is a shift from static guardrails to **runtime, context-aware policy control** (e.g., CDI).
2. Evaluation is moving from outcome-only metrics to **process-integrity metrics**, exposing “corrupt success.”
3. Enterprise papers increasingly favor **deterministic tool/telemetry interfaces** over free-form agent access.
4. Privacy work is maturing from point defenses to **end-to-end pipeline guarantees** (DP across RLHF stages).
5. Security research is still split between **agent-loop controls** and **model/training pipeline hardening**.
6. Memory-enabled agents show capability gains, but also larger failure surfaces from stale/incorrect internal state.
7. Updated misuse studies suggest safeguards help, yet **query decomposition** remains a practical bypass vector.
8. Research infrastructure papers signal demand for **auditable memory/index stacks** for retrieval-heavy agents.
9. **Gap #1:** Few works jointly model permissions, privacy, and memory provenance in one integrated framework.
10. **Gap #2:** Limited real-world longitudinal evaluations under adaptive attackers and evolving tool ecosystems.
